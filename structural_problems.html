IOM's structural problems are bigger than ever: IOM's body grows and grows but the brain has not grown, it seems.

I share the view presented in this thesis.

Essentially, IOM enlarged dramatically in a short period of time (staff, budget, member states, geographical presence, diversification of programatic activities). The current size of Organization with 10,000+ staff and close to 400 offices demanded professional transformation of management and organizational structure, which did not occur. Instead of creating and empowering through proper delegation an amalgamation of professional managers (external labor force) and internal experience, the transformation was entrusted to internal force, who were experienced in their own technical domains, but not being trained for complexity of big systems.

Additional transformational challenge is related to joining the UN system. While projertization is a funding model perfectly suited for "previous IOM" (Agency service oriented), the new UN Migration agency has additional agenda which is not quite "service oriented".

This UN agenda should come with UN funding.

While I am not aware to what extent this happen, the general perception is that the dependance on OSI is a significant one. The field operation are not receiving enough OSI fudning. At the same time they are competing for projects (revenue) with NGOs who have lower infrastructural costs, which creates significant strain on country operations.

The question is now what GSAC can do, or should do?

While recognizing that the GSAC's role/mandate is not to take a part in managing the Organization, i believe that both staff and Administration have the same goal of making the Organization the fest it can be. With that in mind, GSAC should transmit staff views on management impact and issues and Administration should keep ears open. After all, poor management have impact on well-being of GSA members.

My position is also that te GSAC reservations vis-a-vis management directions should be kept internally.
