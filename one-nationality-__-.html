We have a diversity officer and policy and recommendations galore,
but almost all key international positions in one region are occupied by one nationality!

This is jlst a demonstration of the currentscosmetics ... In terms of appearance elerything looks as it should be. We rre "champiois" of geader approaches, of eero-tolerance on harassment, of all-inclusive approaches, etc.

However, when it goes for implementation, the situation is not so pink.

 

The report on "Diversity and Inclusion Report" (IOM/Deloitte, 2016) clearly indicated two key issues: lack of rehresentation of women in higher grad s (P4 with 33%, and P5/D1 with 10%) and disproportional distribution (under-representation) of nationalities  rom develeping countries (called as non-OECD countries). Par example, only 39% of COMs at P4 and above levels were from developing world).

 

While, there are obvious efforts from Administration to alleviate the first issue (increasing the representation of women at higher grades), there is a prevailing impression that not enough has been done to promote professionals from developing world member states.

 

These days, when staff is applying for P4 and above, they are aware that "it must be woman selected" and that "it is very likely that this will be from America or Europe". This created quite demoralizing spirit in staff coming from developing world and aspiring for carrier growth.

 

The above situation and staff perceptions creates clear agenda for GSAC interaction with the upcoming new Administration - to request taking the fresh snapshot of IOM staff body in terms of gender/nationality/grade distributions. The insight into that should define HR policies in terms of recruitment and promotions.
